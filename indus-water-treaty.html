Indus Waters Treaty (1960)

Published Date: August 18, 2025

Background

The Indus Waters Treaty (IWT) was signed in 1960 with the World Bank as a signatory. It is often cited as one of the world’s most durable water‑sharing arrangements. The treaty divides the Indus basin’s six rivers into two groups: India has unrestricted use of the eastern rivers—Ravi, Beas, and Sutlej—while Pakistan receives the western rivers—Indus, Jhelum, and Chenab—subject to specific provisions. This design sought to reduce friction after Partition by providing clarity, predictability, and an institutional framework for cooperation.

How the Treaty Works

While Pakistan has primary rights over the western rivers, India may use them in limited ways for non‑consumptive purposes such as run‑of‑river hydropower, navigation, and certain agricultural uses, provided technical parameters are respected. A permanent Indus Commission enables data exchange, regular meetings, and inspections, acting as the first stop for dispute avoidance and resolution. When differences arise, the treaty outlines escalation from technical consultations to a Neutral Expert or arbitration.

Recurring Disputes and Project Reviews

Over time, several Indian projects on the western rivers—frequently cited examples include the Kishanganga and Ratle hydropower projects—have been scrutinized for compliance with design criteria such as pondage, spillway height, and drawdown levels. These reviews reflect the treaty’s built‑in checks and the high stakes for both countries’ irrigation, energy security, and flood management. Climate variability and glacial dynamics are adding new pressure to an agreement drafted in a different era.

The State of Play in 2025

In 2025, the treaty continues to function, but the political atmosphere remains sensitive. Periodic pauses in technical engagement, divergent interpretations of design rules, and calls for modernizing data‑sharing protocols have kept the treaty in the headlines. Analysts watch for confidence‑building steps—timely data exchange, transparent site visits, and science‑based consultations—that can reduce friction without undermining core provisions.

Pakistan’s Perspective

Pakistan emphasizes that the western rivers are its agricultural lifeline and views strict adherence to treaty limits as essential for food and economic security. Islamabad has frequently urged third‑party facilitation when technical differences persist, arguing that predictable flows and clarity on project designs are non‑negotiable for downstream stability.

India’s Perspective

India maintains that its projects comply with the treaty’s technical envelope and that run‑of‑river hydropower does not materially curtail Pakistan’s entitlements. New Delhi underscores the need to update modalities—especially around technology, safety standards, and real‑time data—to reflect contemporary river science and risk management.

Role of the World Bank

As a signatory, the World Bank supports the treaty’s dispute‑resolution architecture and encourages dialogue when differences arise. Its stance has generally been procedural and facilitative, aimed at keeping discussions technical and treaty‑bound rather than political.

What to Watch Next

Three themes will likely shape the treaty’s next chapter: better data and transparency, climate‑resilient infrastructure, and quicker resolution of technical differences. Progress on these fronts can lower the temperature, protect livelihoods on both sides, and keep a proven framework fit for purpose.

Conclusion

For more than six decades, the Indus Waters Treaty has provided structure in a complex basin shared by two nuclear‑armed neighbors. Its future will depend less on headline politics and more on steady, technical cooperation—sharing data, validating designs, and adapting to a changing climate.
